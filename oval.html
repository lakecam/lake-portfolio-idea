CONTEXT

How it came to be

The organization that I work in is a proudly British Columbian insurance brokerage with over 2,500 employees who has recently undergone a digital awakening as well as a mass acquisition period, buying out other brokerages across the country.

As a UX designer working in the insurance industry myself and having been tasked with researching and analyzing the webpages of not only my own organization, but also those of competitor organizations, it became evident to me, that insurance companies—both the insurance providers and the brokerages—have been competing to be the insurance company of choice for people at the tail end of the millennial generation. While some organizations were able to hone in on a more modern and simplistic aesthetic, my organization adopted a more corporate and legacy aesthetic, which makes sense given that my company was founded in the 1980s, but this visual approach may explain why my organization has not increased its rate of millennial client acquisition. Because of this, I thought it would be an interesting challenge to attempt a branding and responsive web design of a hypothetical insurance company that tried to appeal to millennials and, dare I say, Generation Z folks.

Discovery Research

While conducting discovery interviews with millennials was out of scope for the basis of this project, I had access to demographic research documents within my organization, which indicated a low adoption rate for ‘junior millennials’, an internal term used to denote millennials that are in their 30s and under. The further I dug into the research the more I realized that the competition to appeal to this demographic is fierce, also because of the high number of these ‘junior millennials’ starting families and purchasing their first homes. Because the research I had access to was mainly from a Canadian context, I decided to focus specifically on young Canadian families as the target demographic that this design geared towards.

Brand concept

Since a large part of this project had to do with appealing to young millennial families with a large chunk of it being from a branding perspective, it was logical to start with curating a set of key characteristics to describe the direction this project was heading into. Based on the previous research data, as well as a certain degree of assumptions on my end as an insurance employee, I drafted a concept of what an ideal cross-generational and fiercely Canadian concept could be like. I then came up with key brand characters based on concepts that I drafted.
